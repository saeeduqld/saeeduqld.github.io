You are a consultant with a leading IT consulting firm.  You have a three-month assignment to fill the role as the IS manager (your predecessor resigned) at Confucius College, a university student residence.  Confucius College prides itself on its innovative approaches to the management of the residence that has some 250 student accommodation rooms. During the first and second semesters, the college is normally fully occupied, however, outside these periods occupancy rates fall to between ten and twenty-five percent.  The college is profitable during semester, however, significant losses occur during university recess because most of the college¡¯s costs are fixed (with the exception of cleaning and food). 

Confucius¡¯s profitability had been declining over the last three years and the college management committee was looking for ways to reverse the trend. Accommodation rates could not be raised as it was feared that a drop off in semester occupancy would result (rates were comparable with other colleges). The management committee resolved that the college would rent out its rooms on a short-term basis during university recesses. To help manage this new line of business an information system called CAMS (College Accommodation and Management System) was designed by the college¡¯s IS group (a manager, a programmer/analyst, and a technical and network support specialist). CAMS was designed as an on-line system to run using the college¡¯s Access database system, which was implemented on the college¡¯s network server. The college has quite a large IT infrastructure including network connections to all student rooms, seven management and staff computers, a state of the art library system for the college¡¯s library, and a computer laboratory with 40 state-of-the-art computers.

The go-ahead for the development was given in January 2019, with a view to implementing the system by the end of the year.  The college principal, John Sharp, mentioned this development at the Residential College Managers annual meeting in February 2019.  Two other residences, International Palace and Frobisher House, also thought that this was a good initiative and wanted to join Confucius in the development and implementation of the system.  John Sharp thought this was an excellent opportunity to gain a strategic advantage over the other colleges by allowing them to join the project under the leadership of Confucius.  That way, Confucius could design the system to ensure that its rooms appeared first on the vacancy lists.  After several planning meetings it was agreed that the other two colleges join the project during the requirements stage.  The project was delayed until July 2019 so that personnel from the other colleges could be identified and allocated to the project.  It was also agreed that the system would run centrally at Confucius, using the university network to access the on-line system. 

International Palace and Frobisher were invited to provide input into the design.  International Palace had a large number of international students, and it was their desire to be able to cater to the special dietary requirements of their residents.  They agreed to specify a refectory sub-system that accepted dietary preferences for residents from the Residents database.  The preferences were merged with a menu system that provided detailed menus on a two weeks cycle, specifically integrating residents¡¯ dietary preferences.  The system would produce automatic food purchase orders to relieve the chef¡¯s burden of having to prepare these on a weekly basis. 

Frobisher was a popular residence for Information Systems students.  To save costs, the development was undertaken by a group of Information Systems students resident at the college.  Each group of students was assigned a different module for development.  They were keen to use the system¡¯s development for the requirements of a two-credit point Information Systems case study course.  The construction of the system was to take place during the second semester, 2019, and be complete by the end of the semester in time for assessment of the unit.  The project was handed over to the group to be completed by the end of the semester.

Difficulties obtaining agreement about the specifications for the refectory sub-system had resulted in delays in finalising the functional specifications.  However, to accommodate the needs of the Information Systems student development group, it was agreed to start the system¡¯s construction while the design problems were being resolved, and make any minor modifications once an agreement was reached.  Unfortunately, the difficulties in getting agreement dragged on and the development group completed programming the functionality they thought was best by the end of the semester.  The system turned out to be quite unsuitable.  The developers agreed to redesign the system to cater for the amended requirements.  This couldn¡¯t be done until the start of the New Year because most of the students were returning home for the university vacation. 

The reservations module seemed to work much better, and was implemented for the 2019 summer holidays.  It turned out to be quite a success.  Confucius boasted a seventy percent occupancy, mainly from tour groups of elderly retired singles and couples.  The tour groups made little use of the technical infrastructure.  As students returned for the first semester, they were keen to try out the internet, as access had been arranged through the university network at no extra cost.  Response times for the reservations module deteriorated steadily due to network congestion resulting from internet usage.  So much so, it became impossible to allocate and book student rooms.

The students who originally programmed the system were no longer interested in attempting to resolve the problems with the refectory sub-system, or continuing with further development, because they had completed their project unit assessment.  They suggested some friends may be interested.  When asked about the documentation, they said (with a smile) that it had been handed in for assessment and was no longer available.

One student agreed to make some quick changes to the system so that it could be used.  It was then trialed at International Palace.  Students there soon complained bitterly that the food was not only dreadful, but just not enough of it.  There were reports that the food suppliers were having problems supplying the items ordered because the items ordered were unavailable or out of season.  There also seemed to be some sort of problem with the interface between the reservations system and the menu sub-system.
